Dwarf Planet

Pluto

Overview

Pluto is a dwarf planet in the Kuiper Belt, a donut-shaped region of icy bodies beyond the orbit of Neptune. It was discovered in 1930 by Clyde Tombaugh and was considered the ninth planet in our solar system until its reclassification as a dwarf planet by the International Astronomical Union (IAU) in 2006.Despite its small size and distance from the Sun, Pluto has five known moons, the largest of which is Charon. Pluto and Charon are sometimes considered a binary system due to their similar sizes and close proximity.

Pluto's Atmosphere

Despite being a dwarf planet located far from the Sun, Pluto has a thin atmosphere composed primarily of nitrogen, methane, and carbon monoxide. Studying Pluto's atmosphere and weather patterns can provide insights into its climate dynamics, such as seasonal changes, atmospheric composition variations, and the formation of atmospheric phenomena like hazes and clouds.

New Horizons Mission and Discoveries

The New Horizons spacecraft, launched by NASA in 2006, conducted a historic flyby of Pluto and its moons in 2015, providing the first close-up images and scientific data of the distant dwarf planet. The mission revealed stunning details about Pluto's surface features, including mountains, valleys, and vast icy plains.
